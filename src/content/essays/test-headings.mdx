<span class="dropcap">T</span>his essay exists as a living reference. If you ever forget how a particular formatting element is supposed to work, open `src/content/essays/test-headings.mdx` and copy the pattern. Everything below is a worked example.

The layout uses an automatic table of contents because the frontmatter includes `toc: true`. It is rendered from the H2 and H3 headings on this page.

## A first major section

This is a level-2 heading. They divide an essay into chapters when chapters are not long enough to warrant their own page. Use them generously — they help the reader and they populate the sidebar.

Inside a section you have all the usual prose tools. **Bold for emphasis** and *italic for titles, foreign words, or a softer kind of stress*. You can [link to internal pages](/archive) — these render in the warm brown ink — and you can also [link to external sources](https://situational-awareness.ai/) — these render in dark forest green. The site styles them differently on purpose so the reader knows whether a click leaves the site.

### A subsection with a finer point

Level-3 headings nest inside their parent. They show up in the sidebar indented one level. Use them for genuine sub-divisions, not just because you want to bold a phrase.

#### A sub-subsection for the rare case you need it

Level-4 headings exist for unusually structured pieces — a long methods section, a table that needs internal headings, an enumeration of cases. They render smaller and italic, so the page hierarchy stays legible. **Note**: H4 headings do *not* appear in the TOC sidebar. The sidebar lists only H2 and H3, by design — anything finer would clutter it. If you find yourself reaching for H4 often, the section probably wants to become its own essay or its own series part.

A footnote looks like this in the prose,[^1] and the full reference appears at the very bottom of the essay, with a back-arrow to return you to where you were reading. Multiple footnotes work the same way.[^2]

## Pullquotes for emphasis

When a sentence is doing more work than the surrounding prose, lift it out:

<Pullquote cite="Some attribution, optional">
The past is the only laboratory we have. The next decade may be the most important experiment it has ever run.
</Pullquote>

The pullquote is not a substitute for an argument; it is a way to let one breathe.

## Collapsible explainers

Sometimes a paragraph is a digression that the engaged reader will want and the casual reader will want to skip. The `<Detail>` component gives them a click-to-expand box, so the main thread of the essay stays clean.

<Detail summary="What is Baumol's cost disease, exactly?">
The economist William Baumol observed in 1966 that productivity gains in manufacturing pull wages up across the whole labour market — including in sectors where productivity has not improved. A string quartet still requires four players and forty minutes; a barber still cuts one head of hair at a time. As a consequence, services that resist mechanisation get more expensive over time relative to goods that don't.

This is why a haircut, a violin lesson, and a hospital bed all cost more, in real terms, than they did fifty years ago, even though the underlying activity is essentially unchanged.
</Detail>

You can have multiple expanders in a row:

<Detail summary="And why does this matter for the welfare state?">
Public services are disproportionately the labour-intensive kind. Healthcare, education, eldercare, defence — all dominated by human time. So Baumol's effect bites government budgets harder than private-sector budgets, and it bites them on a recurring basis as productivity in the rest of the economy advances.
</Detail>

## Lists and quotes

Use ordered lists when sequence matters:

1. First, set the frontmatter correctly.
2. Then write the prose.
3. Add components where they earn their place.

And unordered lists when it doesn't:

- Bold for emphasis
- Italic for titles
- Footnotes for sources
- Figures for visuals

A simple block quote, for when you want to drop in someone else's words:

> The function of muscle is to pull and not to push, except in the case of the genitals and the tongue.
> — Leonardo da Vinci

## What this page is not

It is not a published essay. The title is prefixed with **Test:** so it reads as a reference. Keep it published while you are writing — it is faster to copy patterns from a live page than from raw markdown — and either delete it or flip `draft: true` once your own essays cover the same ground.

[^1]: This is the first footnote. Footnotes use GitHub-flavoured markdown syntax and are placed at the bottom of the file. The link in the text and the back-arrow here are wired up automatically.

[^2]: A second footnote, which can be longer than a single sentence. You can include [links inside footnotes](https://situational-awareness.ai/) and they style the same as inline links.
